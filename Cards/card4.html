Warrant 1: Modern ICBMs Discourage Military Escalation

Finnegan Belleau, 2024

Satellite images from last month appeared to show a failed test launch of the RS-28 Sarmat missile, raising concerns about Russia’s nuclear arsenal.

Satellite images taken at the end of September have led arms experts to believe that Russia attempted to test its latest intercontinental ballistic missile, the RS-28 Sarmat, but suffered a catastrophic failure.

The 35-metre-long missile - capable of carrying nuclear warheads - has been referred to as "Satan II" and "the world’s deadliest weapon," heightening concerns due to its destructive capabilities.

"The missile detonated in the silo, leaving a massive crater and destroying the test site," an analyst reported. This could explain the absence of planes supervising test launches, marking the fourth known failure of the RS-28.

The RS-28 is designed to replace the RS-20V Voevoda, a missile over 30 years old. Satellite imagery shows Russia is proceeding with construction to facilitate the missile's deployment.

Since its unveiling in 2014, the RS-28 has faced delays in production and testing. Despite this, it is capable of carrying up to 16 nuclear warheads with a range of at least 18,000 km.

In October 2023, Russian President Vladimir Putin claimed the RS-28 was ready for deployment, emphasizing its purpose to discourage nuclear and military escalation from the United States.

The RS-28 remains central to Russia’s modernization efforts, with deployment underway at several sites despite challenges. Putin described the missile as a key element of Russia's strategic deterrent.
