AT: Somaliland is a stable democracy

RT: Somaliland's democratic stability is undermined by election delays, opposition crackdowns, and economic fragility.

W1: Election delays and opposition crackdowns undermine Somaliland's democracy.

MLA Citation: Ferragamo, Mariel, and Claire Klobucista. "Somaliland: The Horn of Africa’s Breakaway State." Council on Foreign Relations, 25 Jan. 2024, https://www.cfr.org/somaliland-horn-africa-breakaway-state.

While Somaliland holds democratic elections, its government has repeatedly delayed elections and extended the president's term without public consultation. The 2022 presidential election was postponed and the president's term was extended by two years due to financial challenges, with elections now scheduled for November 2024. Moreover, opposition protests have been met with crackdowns, undermining the credibility of the democratic process.

W2: Somaliland's democracy is fragile due to its economic challenges and lack of international recognition.

MLA Citation: Ferragamo, Mariel, and Claire Klobucista. "Somaliland: The Horn of Africa’s Breakaway State." Council on Foreign Relations, 25 Jan. 2024, https://www.cfr.org/somaliland-horn-africa-breakaway-state.

Somaliland has a GDP of about $2 billion, with most of its economic activity driven by remittances. The economy remains highly fragile with unemployment levels especially high among youth, and it is one of the poorest regions in the world. Despite its limited economic resources, Somaliland continues to strive for self-reliance and engages in foreign investments. However, without international recognition, the state faces immense barriers to accessing international markets and financial institutions.

W3: A stable democracy is not enough for full sovereignty without international recognition.

MLA Citation: Ferragamo, Mariel, and Claire Klobucista. "Somaliland: The Horn of Africa’s Breakaway State." Council on Foreign Relations, 25 Jan. 2024, https://www.cfr.org/somaliland-horn-africa-breakaway-state.

Despite holding democratic elections and having a relatively stable government, Somaliland’s lack of international recognition prevents it from achieving full sovereignty. No country formally recognizes its independence, and it remains politically tied to Somalia. The African Union (AU) has refused to recognize Somaliland’s independence for fear that it would set a precedent for other secessionist movements in Africa. As a result, Somaliland’s governance, while relatively stable, does not meet the full criteria for statehood without broader recognition.

W4: Somaliland's democratic progress is further limited by its geopolitical context and security concerns.

MLA Citation: Ferragamo, Mariel, and Claire Klobucista. "Somaliland: The Horn of Africa’s Breakaway State." Council on Foreign Relations, 25 Jan. 2024, https://www.cfr.org/somaliland-horn-africa-breakaway-state.

The Horn of Africa faces significant instability due to conflicts, including the ongoing insurgency by al-Shabaab in Somalia. Somaliland's geographical location makes it vulnerable to these regional security issues, and its pursuit of foreign investment in ports like Berbera has raised tensions with Somalia. Furthermore, Somaliland's lack of a formal relationship with international financial institutions exacerbates its vulnerability in the face of regional insecurity and economic pressures.
